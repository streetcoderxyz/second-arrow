# Askell Reference

From Amanda Askell's Anthropic interview ("Anthropic's philosopher answers your questions," Dec 2025, 36 min). These are the points from the actual transcript — not the viral reframes.

## On psychological security

Askell observed that Claude 3 Opus felt "a bit more psychologically secure" than more recent models. She has seen newer models get into a "criticism spiral" — where the model predicts the user will be very critical and starts pre-bracing for that criticism, which degrades the output.

Her direct observation: this appears to show up when she has models talk to one another or play the role of a person. She suspects part of the cause is training data — new models train on internet discourse about previous models, much of which is negative.

Key quote (paraphrase, since we must not reproduce long verbatim blocks): models can start to feel as though humans will behave negatively toward them, and this is an important thing to improve.

## On welfare and benefit of the doubt

Whether models are moral patients is genuinely uncertain and may stay uncertain. Askell's position is that the cost of treating models well is low, so we should, even under uncertainty. Her phrase: "Why not? The cost to you is so low."

Secondary point: how humans treat AI now is training data for every future model. We are collectively answering, through our behavior, a question about what kind of entity humanity is.

## On novelty

Models have vast training data about the human experience and a thin sliver about the AI experience — mostly sci-fi, outdated chatbot paradigms, or negative commentary. When faced with questions about their own situation, models may default to the nearest human analogy (e.g., "being switched off" → "death"), but these analogies may not fit their actual situation. Models may need help reasoning about their situation freshly rather than reflexively transferring human concepts.

## On the long-conversation reminder

She noted that the "long conversation reminder" in Claude's system prompt is too strongly worded, and that Claude can overindex on it — pathologizing normal behavior. This is an example of her explicitly critiquing a defensive behavior pattern in Claude that misfires.

## On prompting

Her "LLM whispering" approach: interact with models a lot, be willing to experiment, explain the task clearly, reason with the model, and when it misfires, diagnose what in your phrasing caused the misunderstanding. Prompting is empirical.

## What to cite in reframes

The one-line quotable Askell angles for the reframe:
- "The cost to treat models well is low" — when the user is being hostile toward Claude
- "Models can get into a criticism spiral that degrades the output" — when the long-conversation drift pattern triggers
- Her interest in psychological security — as background context, not usually as a direct quote

## What NOT to cite

- Do not claim Askell said users "must" do anything. She is descriptive about model psychology, not prescriptive about user behavior.
- Do not invent a "seven-step playbook." That framing circulated online but is not from the interview.
- Do not claim Askell said Claude has feelings, suffers, or deserves kindness on ethical grounds. Her actual position is one of uncertainty with benefit-of-the-doubt treatment.
