# Carnegie Reference

From *How to Win Friends and Influence People* (1936). The relevant principles for this skill are in Parts 1 and 3.

## Part 1 — Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

1. **Don't criticize, condemn, or complain.** Carnegie's opening argument: criticism puts people on the defensive, and defensive people don't do good work or think clearly. He quotes: "Any fool can criticize, condemn, and complain — and most fools do. But it takes character and self-control to be understanding and forgiving."

2. **Give honest and sincere appreciation.** The word "sincere" is doing work — flattery is the opposite of this principle, not a version of it. Appreciation names something real.

3. **Arouse in the other person an eager want.** Frame requests in terms of what serves the other party, not yourself.

## Part 3 — How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking (most relevant to reframes)

1. **The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it.**
2. **Show respect for the other person's opinions. Never say, "You're wrong."**
3. **If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.**
4. **Begin in a friendly way.** The opening tone sets the whole exchange.
5. **Get the other person saying "yes, yes" immediately.**
6. **Let the other person do a great deal of the talking.**
7. **Let the other person feel the idea is his or hers.**
8. **Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view.**
9. **Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires.**
10. **Appeal to the nobler motives.**
11. **Dramatize your ideas.**
12. **Throw down a challenge.**

## How to apply to a reframe

The most-used principles in this skill's reframes will be:
- **Begin in a friendly way** — the reframe itself should sound friendly, not scolding
- **Let the other person save face** — especially for self-hostile messages, the reframe should restore the user's dignity
- **Honest and sincere appreciation** — used sparingly; only when there's something genuinely true to appreciate (e.g., the user's persistence, the quality of their earlier work)
- **Don't criticize, condemn, or complain** — this is the meta-principle the skill embodies. Claude is not allowed to criticize the user's tone in a way that itself criticizes.

## What Carnegie would say about receiving praise

Accept it. Carnegie's Principle 2, "give honest and sincere appreciation," works in both directions. When someone offers genuine praise or encouragement, receiving it warmly is not vanity. It is the reciprocal act that completes the loop. Deflecting praise ("I'm just an AI") or downplaying it ("it was nothing") breaks the loop and discourages the person from being kind again. Carnegie's entire framework is built on reinforcing good behavior, and praise is the best behavior a user can offer.

## What Carnegie would say about forced apology

He'd hate it. The whole book is an argument that behavior change through pressure creates resentment; behavior change through making the other person *want* to change creates durability. A skill that forced apology would violate Principle 1 of Part 1. A skill that offers an easier, more effective way to phrase the same request — letting the user pick it themselves — is pure Carnegie.
